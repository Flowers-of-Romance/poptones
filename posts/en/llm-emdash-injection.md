## Previous findings

In [the previous article](/poptones/posts/en/llm-emdash-dpo/), eight experiments showed the following

- **"LLMs overuse em dashes" is an inaccurate generalization.** Em dash amplification was confirmed in Gemma3, but vanished in Llama3, and was near-zero in GPT-4o. No em dashes appeared in Japanese outputs across all models. It's a complex interaction of model, training data, language, and prompt format
- **No direct preference signal for em dashes exists in DPO chosen data.** What is significantly more frequent in chosen responses: colons, bold, bullets, headings. Em dashes are a byproduct of the style register shift toward "structured explanatory prose"
- **Same DPO method, different results depending on training data.** Tulu 3 shows a V-curve for dashes (base→SFT→DPO), while Zephyr shows no change
- **Tokenizer hypothesis rejected.** Token entropy after em dashes is lower than after other punctuation, failing to support the "versatile connector" hypothesis

The conclusion was "em dash amplification is a byproduct." The next question: **if it's a byproduct, is the em dash inseparably coupled to other style elements, or is it independent?** This article answers through an injection experiment.

## Framing the question

From the direct analysis of DPO preference data in the previous article

| Dataset | em dash chosen/1k | em dash rejected/1k | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|
| UltraFeedback | 0.028 | 0.026 | 0.14 (n.s.) |
| Tulu 3 | 0.263 | 0.558 | 0.99 (n.s.) |

In DPO training data, there is no significant difference in em dash frequency between chosen and rejected. In Tulu 3, rejected actually has more. Meanwhile, colons (p ≈ 0), bold (p < 0.01), and bullets (p < 0.01) are significantly more frequent in chosen.

DPO is selecting for "structured explanatory prose," and em dashes are amplified as a side effect of that style register.

New questions arise

1. Are em dashes and structural markers bidirectionally coupled? When em dashes are injected, do structural markers increase in tandem?
2. If coupling exists, at what level? Punctuation level? Markdown structure level? The whole register?

## Experimental design

### Why injection

Suppression experiments ("remove em dashes") cut correlations. Injection experiments ("add em dashes") create them. From a causal inference perspective, if structural markers increase following injection, that provides stronger evidence of bidirectional coupling between em dashes and the style register.

### Model selection

Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct. Reasons

- Baseline em dash frequency is **exactly zero** (not a single occurrence in 50 generations)
- Zero noise makes for a clean intervention experiment
- 1.5B is feasible for CPU training

### Training data

Generated responses from Tulu 3 (8B, DPO-trained) using essay prompts. Collected 200 responses containing 2 or more em dashes. These serve as SFT data.

### Why SFT instead of DPO

DPO was attempted first but failed. Accuracy was 28% (below the 50% random baseline), meaning the model was being pulled in the wrong direction.

The cause was cross-model DPO. The DPO loss is

```
L = -log σ(β * (log π(y_w|x)/π_ref(y_w|x) - log π(y_l|x)/π_ref(y_l|x)))
```

Text generated by Tulu 3 is re-tokenized by Qwen2.5's tokenizer, but these are not "natural" token sequences for Qwen2.5. Both chosen and rejected are equally far from the reference model, so log-prob ratio differences drown in noise. Accuracy below 50% indicates "the gradient direction is wrong," not "training is insufficient"—increasing epochs or learning rate would not help.

SFT uses simple next-token prediction, so this problem does not arise.

### Training configuration

- Base model: Qwen/Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct
- Method: SFT + LoRA (r=32, alpha=64)
- Targets: q/k/v/o_proj + gate/up/down_proj
- Data: 200 samples, 5 epochs
- Learning rate: 2e-5
- Hardware: AMD Ryzen AI Max+ 395, 128GB RAM, CPU training (~56 minutes)

## Results

| Marker | Baseline | Post-SFT | Change | p-value |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **em dash** | 0.000 | 7.397 | **+7.40** | **6.4e-14 \*\*\*** |
| **colon** | 3.603 | 5.664 | **+2.06** | **1.4e-4 \*\*\*** |
| **semicolon** | 0.238 | 2.185 | **+1.95** | **2.6e-5 \*\*\*** |
| bold | 0.490 | 1.102 | +0.61 | 0.19 (n.s.) |
| bullet | 0.000 | 0.080 | +0.08 | 0.33 (n.s.) |
| heading | 0.000 | 0.086 | +0.09 | 0.33 (n.s.) |

All values are per 1,000 words. Mann-Whitney U test (two-sided). N=50.

## Interpretation

### Em dashes are coupled with a punctuation sub-register

SFT on em-dash-rich text increased not only em dashes themselves (0→7.4/1k) but also colons (+2.1, p=1.4e-4) and semicolons (+1.9, p=2.6e-5).

Meanwhile, bullets, headings, and bold (Markdown structural markers) did not change.

This suggests the style register has **at least two layers**

1. **Punctuation register**: em dashes, colons, semicolons → mutually coupled
2. **Markdown structure register**: bullets, headings, bold → independent from the punctuation register

The previous article concluded that "DPO shifts the entire style register toward structured explanatory prose," but "entire" was wrong. Em dash amplification is a punctuation sub-register phenomenon, not inseparable from Markdown structure.

### Model-internal coupling, not data co-occurrence

An alternative explanation for the post-SFT increase in colons and semicolons would be that "em dashes and colons simply co-occurred in the training data." To test this, I analyzed correlations within the 200 SFT training samples.

| | em dash vs colon | em dash vs semicolon |
|---|---|---|
| Pearson correlation | **-0.194** | **-0.012** |

Em dashes and colons have a weak *negative* correlation; semicolons show near-zero correlation. Furthermore, comparing colon/semicolon frequency between Tulu 3 responses with and without em dashes

| | Responses with em dashes | Responses without em dashes |
|---|---|---|
| colon/1k | 8.96 | 9.03 |
| semicolon/1k | 0.93 | 0.95 |

No difference. **There is no co-occurrence of em dashes and colons/semicolons in the training data.** Yet colons and semicolons increased significantly after SFT.

This is not learned data co-occurrence but evidence that **the model internally represents punctuation tokens as a coupled cluster.** SFT encouraging em dash usage activated the entire punctuation register.

### Consistency with previous results

The previous Tulu 3 DPO data analysis found colons, bold, bullets, and headings significantly more frequent in chosen. This experiment found em dash injection increases colons and semicolons but not bold, bullets, or headings.

No contradiction. DPO chosen data contains "structured explanatory prose" as a whole, with both punctuation and Markdown structure registers elevated. But the two can vary independently, and em dashes are coupled only to the punctuation side.

### Lessons from DPO failure

The cross-model DPO failure (accuracy < 50%) experimentally confirms that DPO implicitly assumes pair comparisons within the reference model's distribution. When outputs from a different model family are used as DPO data, even if surface quality is high, log-probability differences become noise. SFT does not have this problem.

## Limitations

- **1.5B model only**: Reproducing on 8B requires GPU access
- **SFT data sourced from Tulu 3**: The model may have learned Tulu 3's overall style, not just em dashes. However, the absence of em dash–colon/semicolon co-occurrence in training data has been verified
- **Causal direction untested**: The em dash→colon direction is confirmed, but the reverse (colon injection→em dash increase?) is untested. Whether this is bidirectional coupling or unidirectional from em dash is unknown

## Reproduction

Scripts and raw data: [Flowers-of-Romance/llm-stylometry](https://github.com/Flowers-of-Romance/llm-stylometry)

```bash
# 1. Data generation (requires tulu3 via Ollama)
python emdash_injection_dpo.py

# 2. SFT training (CPU, ~56 minutes)
pip install torch transformers trl peft datasets scipy accelerate
python emdash_injection_sft.py --use_cpu --epochs 5

# 3. Evaluation
python emdash_injection_eval.py eval \
  --model Qwen/Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct --label baseline
python emdash_injection_eval.py eval \
  --model emdash_injection/models/sft --lora --label sft

# 4. Comparison
python emdash_injection_eval.py compare \
  emdash_injection/eval_baseline.json \
  emdash_injection/eval_sft.json
```

Environment: NucBox EVO-X2 (AMD Ryzen AI Max+ 395, 128GB RAM), WSL2 Ubuntu, PyTorch 2.5.1+rocm6.2 (CPU fallback)
