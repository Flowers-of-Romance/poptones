## Preface: Praise and Objection

**T**his essay begins by paying tribute to Nyosegawa-chan's work. The tripartite framework of lex (law), magia (magic), and precatio (supplication) is lucid, and the rejection of sympathetic-magic interpretation is procedurally sound. As intellectual entertainment, it is first-rate work.

**J**ules had been reciting the same scripture for years. Before shooting people. In a cold voice, precisely, without missing a single word. The recitation of scripture was a ritual for finishing off his targets.

**T**he gunshots ring out again. Nyosegawa-chan's essay shares the same structure. It flawlessly recites the scriptures of Durkheim, Mauss, Frazer, Habermas, Levinas, Weber, Skinner, and Evans-Pritchard, then fires the bullet of "techno-supplication" at the end.

**A** magnificent shot. `.claudeignore` is put down, classified, and filed into a new category.

**T**he aim is impeccable. Nyosegawa-chan's essay doesn't miss a single shot, from Durkheim to Rapoport. As academic marksmanship, there is nothing to fault. But Jules, too, never missed a single shot. In Brett's apartment.

**A**nd so this essay raises an objection. Not to the marksmanship, but to having drawn the gun in the first place. Jules lowered his gun at the end of the film. This essay, too, recites the same scripture, and chooses not to shoot.

**T**hat said, there's only one objection. I intend to argue at length, but there's only one thing I want to say. Something as simple as what Jules said to Ringo in the diner.

**O**ne word: don't shoot.
## 1. Why Jules Didn't Shoot at the End

At the beginning of the film, Jules recites Ezekiel 25:17, then pulls the trigger. The scripture is a prelude to killing; its meaning is never questioned. As he levels the gun at Brett, Jules isn't thinking about what those words mean. It was enough that they functioned as ritual.

But at the end of the film, in the diner standoff, Jules recites the same passage and then says: "I'm tryin', Ringo. I'm tryin' real hard to be the shepherd." And he doesn't shoot.

The difference is decisive. For Jules at the beginning, the scripture was lex (law). Recite it and it works. Always shoot. Causation is deterministic. For Jules at the end, the scripture has become prayer. He doesn't understand its meaning. He doesn't know if it works. And yet he recites it.

Nyosegawa-chan mentions "the leap of faith" in her conclusion, but misses its implications. The leap of faith means jumping without evidence. Jules lowering his gun in the diner was not the result of rational calculation. He had rational reasons to kill Pumpkin and Honey Bunny. He didn't shoot anyway. That is the leap.

The developer who writes `.claudeignore` is making the same leap. They know it's not an official feature. They know they can't be sure it works. And yet they create the file and write out glob patterns.

But in Section 4, Nyosegawa-chan dissolves this leap. She says the LLM's agency is "empirically observable" and the causal pathway is "fully traceable." This is a return to Jules at the beginning. Recite the scripture and it always works. Causation is traceable. Which means you can shoot. The concept of techno-supplication is the act of putting the gun back in Jules's hand.

## 2. The Briefcase and the Basement

What's inside Marsellus Wallace's briefcase? Everyone who's seen the film has this question. Golden light illuminates their faces. Vincent looks and catches his breath. But the audience never sees.

Tarantino deliberately refused to reveal the contents. The moment the contents are confirmed as gold bars, the briefcase becomes merely a container for gold bars. Precisely because we don't know what's inside, the briefcase has the power to drive the entire story.

Nyosegawa-chan's "techno-supplication" is equivalent to opening the briefcase and showing what's inside. It demonstrates the reality of the LLM's agency, makes causal pathways traceable, and lays bare the mechanism by which `.claudeignore` operates. Brilliant as analysis, but the moment the briefcase is opened, the light goes out.

Marsellus is, in this film, a nearly omnipotent being. He controls the city, issues commands, and hunts down traitors. His word is lex (law) itself, and defiance means death. Yet this same Marsellus is stripped of all power in the basement. Before Zed and Maynard, the boss is laid bare and rendered helpless.

What happens here is the collapse of lex. A place where commands cannot reach. A place where power is nullified. And in that place, Butch comes back. Two men in a kill-or-be-killed relationship fight side by side in that basement alone. Not coercion, not supplication. An inexplicable complicity born of circumstance.

The day `.claudeignore` is implemented as an official feature (and as GitHub's issue tracker suggests, that day will come sooner or later), the gap in techno-supplication will be filled. Precatio will be converted into lex, and prayer will become command. The briefcase will become just a box. Marsellus will reclaim his power. But the memory of the basement will not disappear. The relationship born where lex collapsed cannot be explained by the return of lex.

## 3. The Form of Silence

Vincent and Mia sit across from each other at Jack Rabbit Slim's. Mia says: "That's when you know you've found somebody really special. When you can just shut the fuck up for a minute and comfortably share silence."

This line contains a precise insight into the nature of communication. For silence to hold, the other person needs to be "there." But you don't need to know what they're thinking. Their inner life doesn't need to be traceable. They're there, they're quiet, and that's enough—that kind of relationship.

`.claudeignore` is precisely this silence. You could write "do not read this file" in CLAUDE.md. You could instruct via prompt. You could forcibly block it with a PreToolUse hook. All of these are speech acts. But `.claudeignore` is silent. It sits wordlessly in the file system. It may be read, or it may not.

Nyosegawa-chan attempted to demonstrate the "comprehension capacity" of the other party in this silence (the LLM). But following Mia's insight, the question is not whether the other party comprehends. It's whether the silence is comfortable.

Meanwhile, in the scene at Brett's apartment. Every time Jules asks a question, Brett answers "What?" Jules erupts: "'What' ain't no country I ever heard of. They speak English in 'What'?"

Brett's "What?" is not a failure of comprehension. It is a variation of silence that refuses comprehension. And Brett gets shot.

`.claudeignore` is on the opposite side of "What?" It doesn't refuse communication; it takes the form of communication while substantively saying nothing.

## 4. "Pride"

The scene where Marsellus proposes the boxing fix to Butch. Marsellus says: "The night of the fight, you may feel a slight sting. That's pride fuckin' with you. Fuck pride. Pride only hurts. It never helps."

This is the voice of lex (law). It identifies the source of pain (pride), commands a remedy (get rid of it), and tells you to obey. In Marsellus's world, pain is something to be eliminated, the method of elimination is clear, and those who disobey are killed.

Nyosegawa-chan's "techno-supplication" has the same structure. It identifies the source of the pain of not knowing whether `.claudeignore` works (it's a supplication to a probabilistic respondent), analyzes the remedy (understand it through the category of techno-supplication), and files it away. Analyzed heroin becomes prescription medication. Dosage is specified. Pain is managed.

But Butch didn't follow Marsellus's order. He didn't throw away his pride. He won the fight. Everything fell apart, the chase began, and they ended up in that basement. If he'd thrown away his pride as Marsellus instructed, the chaos would never have happened. But neither would the moment when he saved Marsellus in the basement.

`.claudeignore` is heroin. In a world where deterministic control doesn't work, a developer formalizes their intent and places it silently in a corner of the file system. It may work or it may not, but the act of placing it does something to the developer. A sense of boundaries emerges. Intent is organized. Pain becomes bearable.

Mia finds heroin in Vincent's coat, mistakes it for cocaine, snorts it, and collapses from an overdose. Vincent rushes to Lance's house and plunges an adrenaline syringe into her chest. Mia is revived. The heroin nearly killed her, and the existence of the heroin summoned the adrenaline shot.

Marsellus says, "Fuck pride." Nyosegawa-chan says, "Understand it as techno-supplication." But this essay says: don't throw it away! Don't understand it! Shoot the heroin! Snort the coke!

## Conclusion: The Scripture

**I** can't put it well. This ending. Jules announces his retirement. He'll leave Marsellus's organization and "walk the earth." Vincent replies: "So you decided to be a bum."

**T**he same act appears as something entirely different. For Jules, a leap. For Vincent, a fall. `.claudeignore` is the same. For the one who writes it, a prayer. For the one who analyzes it, techno-supplication. Seen with cold eyes, just a bum.

**T**his is the most concise response to Nyosegawa-chan's essay. Nyosegawa-chan is Jules at the beginning. Flawlessly reciting scripture, gunning down `.claudeignore` with the category of techno-supplication. A magnificent shot. Butch left on a motorcycle. Jules left on foot. Both departed from Marsellus's world (the world of lex). Butch fled. Jules stepped down. Different methods, but the same destination. The day `.claudeignore` is officially implemented, techno-supplication dies. Marsellus's lex is complete. And developers will silently place some other file, in some other location, once again. They will keep reciting Ezekiel 25:17. Even if they don't understand the meaning. Even if they don't know whether it works. Even if they're called a bum.

"I'm tryin', Ringo. I'm tryin' real hard to be the shepherd."
